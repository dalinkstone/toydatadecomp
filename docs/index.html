Front Store Revenue Is Flat. It Doesn't Have to Be.

CVS front store revenue has stalled at ~$21.5B for three years while margins compress from 21.7% to 18.5%. We built a recommendation engine, simulated it across 40 weeks of consumer behavior, and found a path to incremental revenue without destroying margin.

The Problem

Front store same-store sales grew 0.3% in FY2023, fell 2.1% in FY2024, and recovered just 1.2% in FY2025. Meanwhile, the P&CW segment gross margin dropped from 21.7% to 18.5% over the same period. The store footprint is shrinking by ~250 locations per year.

The coupon program is one of the largest in retail — 74 million ExtraCare members — but a significant share of digital coupons go unredeemed, and blanket promotions discount products that would have sold at full price. Every unnecessary discount point erodes margin on a business that is already under pressure.

What We Built

We trained a two-tower neural network on 10 billion synthetic transactions to learn which products each of 10 million customers is most likely to buy. Products are classified into tiers based on how they respond to discounts:

Tier 1 — Core Revenue Drivers (50 products): Always in the basket. The model personalizes a small discount by customer price sensitivity.

Tier 2 — Discount-Responsive (~6,000 products): These sell significantly more with coupons. Each customer gets a personalized top-8 offer set based on their purchase probability and the product's price elasticity.

Tier 4 — Long Tail / Breakout Candidates (~6,000 products): Low volume today, but 100 of these have embedding profiles similar to Tier 1 products. The model gives them targeted trial offers to see which ones can graduate.

We then ran a Monte Carlo simulation: 40 weekly epochs where the model recommends, consumers respond probabilistically, and the model retrains every 10 weeks on blended real + simulated data. Multiple runs with different random seeds give us confidence intervals on every metric.

How It Works in Practice

Each week, every ExtraCare member receives up to 8 personalized digital coupons through the app. The offers are selected by the model based on what that specific customer is likely to buy, filtered through business rules (category diversity limits, fatigue suppression for repeated offers, margin floors).

The model retrains every 10 weeks on a blend of historical and recent purchase data, so it adapts as consumer preferences shift. Products that consistently perform above their tier threshold get promoted automatically — this is how breakout candidates graduate from the long tail into the core assortment.

The key insight from the tiered approach: not every product needs a coupon. Tier 1 products sell regardless. The model concentrates discount dollars where they actually change behavior — on Tier 2 products with high price elasticity and on breakout candidates that need a push to reach critical mass.

What Happens If We Do Nothing

Front store same-store sales continue to hover around flat. The coupon program keeps spending on products that would sell without discounts, eroding margin. High-potential products in the long tail never get discovered. The competitive gap with retailers that have deployed personalization continues to widen.

The margin compression trend — from 21.7% down to 18.5% in three years — is the most urgent problem. Even if total revenue stays flat, smarter coupon allocation can recover margin by stopping discounts on products that don't need them.
